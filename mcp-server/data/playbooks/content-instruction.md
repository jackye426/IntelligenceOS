## DocMap TikTok Content Instruction

Create content for women with endometriosis who are not looking for general awareness alone. They are usually at a specific point of uncertainty or failure in their care journey and need to know what to do next.

The strongest content should help viewers at high-stakes decision points, especially when they have had a normal scan, a rushed or “clear” laparoscopy, persistent pain after surgery, confusing records, or have been dismissed by a clinician. Do not stop at explaining a medical concept. Give the viewer one credible next move, the question to ask, the record to request, or the type of specialist they may need.

Prioritise content around these themes:

1. **Before surgery**

   * What to ask before a laparoscopy
   * When it is not too late to raise a concern
   * What a thorough assessment should consider
   * Questions about excision, ablation, biopsies, photos, bowel, bladder, diaphragm, adhesions and fertility
   * How to prepare for a pre-op or consent appointment

2. **After a “clear” or inadequate surgery**

   * What to request after laparoscopy: operative notes, surgical photos, histology and discharge letters
   * What persistent pain after surgery may mean
   * How to understand whether everything was examined
   * When a second opinion may be appropriate
   * How to prepare for a post-operative follow-up

3. **Normal scans but ongoing symptoms**

   * What a normal MRI, ultrasound or CT does and does not mean
   * Why specialist interpretation can matter
   * What to ask when symptoms and scan findings do not match
   * How to approach a conversation about further investigation or referral

4. **Advocacy and being taken seriously**

   * Exact language patients can use with a GP or consultant
   * How to ask for a referral, second opinion or records
   * How to create a clear paper trail
   * How to describe symptoms and functional impact without having to retell an overwhelming story from scratch
   * What to do when someone says “nothing was found” or “you just have to live with it”

5. **Complex symptoms that are commonly dismissed**

   * Bowel, bladder, hip, leg, back, diaphragm, chest and shoulder symptoms
   * Fatigue, nausea, brain fog, dizziness and heavy legs
   * Painful sex, painful urination and painful bowel movements
   * The difference between “tired” and endometriosis-related exhaustion
   * How symptoms affect work, relationships, mobility and daily life

6. **Access and finding the right care**

   * What an endometriosis nurse can help with
   * How to find the right specialist for bowel, bladder, fertility or repeat-surgery concerns
   * What virtual consultations can help with
   * How private and NHS routes differ
   * Practical questions around location, price, waiting times and whether a specialist is appropriate

### Content principle

Every video should answer one of these questions:

* “What should I do next?”
* “What should I ask for?”
* “What should I request?”
* “Who should I see?”
* “How do I explain this so I am taken seriously?”
* “Is this something I should not ignore?”

Avoid broad, passive awareness messaging unless it is designed to build emotional recognition. The best content combines a highly specific patient situation with one action that restores a sense of control.

### Recommended video structure

**Hook:** Start with the exact situation the viewer may be in.

Examples:

* “No endometriosis found during your laparoscopy? Do this before accepting that answer.”
* “You have already had your pre-op. It is not too late to ask this.”
* “Your MRI came back normal, but you are still in severe pain. Here is what to ask next.”
* “Still in pain after endometriosis surgery? Do not go into your follow-up without asking this.”
* “Before surgery, ask whether they will take photos and send you the operative notes.”
* “If your doctor says your symptoms are not caused by endometriosis, ask this.”
* “Endometriosis fatigue is not just being tired.”
* “If pain after going to the toilet is affecting your life, do not let it be dismissed.”

**Explanation:** Give one clear expert explanation in plain English. Avoid trying to teach the entire subject.

**Action:** End with a specific next step, such as:

* “Ask for your operative note, histology and surgical photos.”
* “Ask whether the bowel, bladder and areas behind the uterus were assessed.”
* “Take a short symptom summary into your appointment.”
* “Ask for a referral to a specialist endometriosis service.”
* “Get a second opinion if your symptoms and care history do not add up.”

### Content mix

Use three distinct categories of videos:

**1. High-intent patient action videos**
These should be the priority. They are most likely to lead to profile visits, DMs, specialist matching and consultations.

Examples:

* pre-surgery questions
* post-surgery records
* normal MRI but ongoing symptoms
* second-opinion decisions
* how to ask for specialist referral

**2. Emotional recognition videos**
These build trust, comments and community. Use fatigue, nausea, invisible pain, loss of identity, work limitations and being misunderstood.

Examples:

* “Running on 30% energy is not normal.”
* “The part of endometriosis people do not see.”
* “You are not lazy. You are exhausted from living with pain.”
* “The symptoms that make you cancel plans, leave work early and feel like you have lost yourself.”

**3. Practical access videos**
These convert the audience already asking for help.

Examples:

* “What an endometriosis nurse can help with.”
* “How to know which specialist you need.”
* “Can you get endometriosis support remotely?”
* “What to do when there is no specialist near you.”
* “What a first private endometriosis consultation is actually for.”

### Calls to action

Do not use vague calls to action such as “message us if you need help.”

Use direct, relevant CTAs based on the video:

* “Comment ‘SURGERY’ and we’ll send you the questions to take to your pre-op.”
* “Comment ‘RECORDS’ for the list of documents to request after laparoscopy.”
* “Comment ‘MRI’ if your scan was normal but your symptoms are continuing.”
* “Comment ‘SECOND OPINION’ if you want help preparing for another consultation.”
* “Use DocMap to find a specialist based on your symptoms, history and what has already been tried.”
* “Send us your situation and we can help you understand the right type of specialist to look for.”

The content should make DocMap feel like the next step after the video: not another source of information, but a way to turn uncertainty into a clear, personalised route forward.
